This is a payments workload, about to ship to a Kubernetes cluster. The YAML is valid, the pipeline is green. But valid is not safe. Let's ask KubeGuard what an attacker sees.
Beat one. Detect. One command reads the manifests and reasons over them. Twenty-one findings — four critical: a privileged container, a host-path mount, and two bindings handing a service account cluster-admin.
Beat two — and this is what sets KubeGuard apart. Chain. It doesn't just list problems; it links them into one ATT&CK-tagged attack path. Internet-facing service, to container escape, to the node, to a harvestable token, to full cluster-admin. That's how a checkout page becomes a cluster takeover.
Beat three. Prioritize. Every finding gets a deterministic, explainable score — no black box. The cluster-admin bindings and the privileged container top the list, because they're attack-path enablers, internet-exposed, and blast-radius wide. This is your fix-first list.
Beat four. Harden. KubeGuard doesn't stop at the diagnosis — it writes the cure. A least-privilege bundle: pod-security, a default-deny network policy, scoped RBAC, admission policies. Apply it, re-scan, and the count goes to zero. Detect, to fix, to proof — one loop.
Beat five. Prove it to an auditor. The same scan maps every finding to eleven compliance frameworks — C-I-S, N-I-S-T 800-53, P-C-I, I-S-O 27001, and regional ones — honestly labelled as indicative, not a certification. And a fail-on gate returns a non-zero exit, so this blocks a pull request in CI.
From a pile of misconfigurations, to a single ATT&CK-tagged attack path, to a fix bundle that scans clean. Offline, read-only, safe to run against production today. KubeGuard. Open source, Apache 2.0.
